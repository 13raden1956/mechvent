Introduction to Mechanical Ventilation

A framework-based introduction to ventilator mechanics, modes, and advanced NIV concepts

Ventilator anatomy & physiology
How ICU and transport/ED ventilators work mechanically
ICU ventilator
Microprocessor-driven flow control valves on both inspiratory and expiratory limbs allow precise, real-time modulation of gas delivery. Gas is drawn from wall-piped O₂ and air blended at the machine.
Pneumatic source
High-pressure wall gas (50 psi); internal blender
Flow control
Proportional solenoid/servo valve — millisecond feedback loop
Expiratory valve
Active PEEP valve; adjustable to maintain end-expiratory pressure
Monitoring
Continuous flow, pressure, and volume waveforms; loops; alarms
Trigger sensitivity
Flow-trigger (≥1–3 L/min) or pressure-trigger; highly responsive
Examples
PB 980/840, Draeger Evita, Hamilton G5/C6
ED / transport ventilator
Simpler pneumatic or turbine-driven systems optimized for portability and durability. Fewer modes, coarser flow control, often lacking advanced waveform analytics.
Pneumatic source
Compressed O₂ cylinder (turbine models can use ambient air)
Flow control
Fixed-orifice pneumatics or blower turbine — less precise modulation
Expiratory valve
Spring-loaded passive or simple threshold PEEP valve
Monitoring
Basic pressure gauge ± SpO₂; limited waveform display
Trigger sensitivity
Often pressure-trigger only; higher thresholds → more trigger work
Examples
LTV 1200, ReVel, Zoll 731
The breath cycle — four universal phases

Inspiration (limit governs)
Cycle off
Expiration (passive)
Every mechanical breath — regardless of mode — passes through four phases: trigger → inspiration → cycle → expiration. The ventilator opens the inspiratory valve, gas flows into the lungs, a cycling criterion terminates inspiration, and the expiratory valve opens passively. PEEP is maintained by the threshold expiratory valve.
Key mechanical difference: ICU ventilators recalculate flow targets at every 2–5 ms sample interval, enabling breath-to-breath adaptation, pressure-volume loop display, and leak compensation. Transport vents deliver a fixed breath profile — safer for a moving vehicle but far less adaptable to changing patient mechanics.

Clinical pearl: When a patient is transported from the ED to the ICU, always re-evaluate ventilator settings on arrival. Transport vents often mask high peak pressures, auto-PEEP, and patient-ventilator dyssynchrony that become apparent only on ICU-grade waveform monitoring.

Trigger · Limit · Cycle
The universal language of mechanical ventilation — applies to every mode and every ventilator
Trigger
What starts the breath?
The signal — patient effort or timer — that opens the inspiratory valve.
Limit
What constrains inspiration?
The maximum value of flow or pressure that cannot be exceeded during inspiration.
Cycle
What ends the breath?
The criterion — volume, time, flow %, or pressure — that terminates inspiration.
Trigger — starting the breath
Time trigger (mandatory)
The ventilator timer expires (e.g., set rate 12/min → breath every 5 sec). No patient effort required. Used in fully controlled modes or apnoeic patients.
Patient trigger (assisted)
Patient effort detected as either a pressure drop below PEEP (pressure trigger, –1 to –2 cmH₂O) or an inspiratory flow deflection (flow trigger, 1–3 L/min). Flow triggering reduces trigger work by ~50%.

Clinical relevance: Too sensitive → auto-triggering (cardiac oscillations, water in circuit). Too insensitive → increased work of breathing and fatigue.

Limit — governing the inspiratory phase
The limit variable is held at or below a maximum value throughout inspiration — but reaching the limit alone does not end the breath (that is cycling's job).
Flow limit (Volume Control)
Gas delivered at a set constant or decelerating flow. Pressure rises as a consequence. The flow is limited — cannot exceed the set value.
Pressure limit (Pressure Control / PSV)
Inspiratory pressure is capped at a set value above PEEP. Flow varies freely and decelerates as the lung fills. Volume is a consequence.

Clinical relevance: In pressure-limited breaths, tidal volume is not guaranteed — it depends on compliance and resistance. In flow-limited (volume control) breaths, volume is guaranteed but peak pressure varies with mechanics.

Cycle — ending the breath
Volume cycle
Breath ends when a set tidal volume has been delivered. Standard in Volume Control. Guarantees Vt regardless of mechanics.
Time cycle
Breath ends after a set inspiratory time. Used in Pressure Control. Ti is set explicitly — gives the clinician direct control of I:E ratio.
Flow cycle (% peak)
Breath ends when inspiratory flow decays to a threshold (often 25% of peak). Used in PSV, BiPAP, and AVAPS. Tracks the patient's own neural inspiratory effort — most physiologically synchronized.
Pressure cycle
Breath ends if circuit pressure exceeds a safety limit. Acts as a safety ceiling, not a primary cycle mechanism.

Key insight — why flow-cycling matches who is in charge: Flow cycling lets the patient's own neural effort define breath duration. Time cycling lets the clinician define breath duration. Use flow-cycling when the patient drives the breath; time-cycling when they cannot be relied upon to do so.

Why PC is time-cycled and PSV/BiPAP are flow-cycled
In Pressure Control, the clinician is delivering a mandatory breath — possibly to an apneic or paralyzed patient. There is no reliable neural signal to detect. Time-cycling gives the clinician certainty about Ti and I:E ratio.
In PSV and BiPAP, the patient initiated the breath. The decay in inspiratory flow reflects waning neural effort — the patient's own respiratory system signaling "I'm done inspiring." Flow-cycling lets the machine track that biological signal rather than an arbitrary clock.

When the backup rate fires in BiPAP/AVAPS: The mode switches mid-breath from flow-cycled (PSV behavior) to time-cycled (PC behavior). The backup Ti you set becomes the operative cycling mechanism. Set it deliberately — if the patient becomes apneic, the backup Ti is the only thing controlling inspiratory time.

Modes of ventilation
History, meaning, and relationship to trigger · limit · cycle
Volume Control (VC)
History: Volume-cycled ventilation dates to the Emerson post-polio iron lungs of the 1950s and positive-pressure machines of the 1960s. The principle: deliver a fixed tidal volume, let pressure vary. Widely adopted after the ARDSNet trial (2000) demonstrated that targeting Vt 6 mL/kg IBW reduced mortality — a trial conducted in volume control.
PhaseVariableDetail
TriggerTime or patient effortMinimum mandatory rate set; patient can trigger additional breaths above the set rate
LimitFlowSet flow rate (constant square-wave or descending ramp). Pressure is NOT limited — it rises with lung stiffness
CycleVolumeBreath ends when set Vt is delivered. Ti is derived (Vt ÷ Flow) — not directly set or guaranteed

Clinical pearls: VC guarantees Vt — useful for precise lung-protective ventilation and monitoring plateau pressure (Pplat). If compliance worsens acutely, peak/plateau pressures rise. Constant-flow waveform may worsen patient-ventilator dyssynchrony versus decelerating flow.

Common misconception: Ti is not directly set or guaranteed in VC. It is derived from Vt ÷ Flow. If you set 500 mL at 60 L/min, Ti = 0.5 s — but it emerges from those settings, it is not a primary set parameter. Ti is directly set and guaranteed only in Pressure Control.

Pressure Control (PC)
History: Pressure-limited, time-cycled ventilation was pioneered in neonatal care in the 1970s–80s where small uncuffed ETTs made volume delivery imprecise. It gained popularity in adult ARDS management in the 1990s as a means of capping peak airway pressure and generating a more physiological decelerating flow waveform.
PhaseVariableDetail
TriggerTime or patient effortMandatory rate set; patient triggering allowed
LimitPressure (ΔP)Set driving pressure above PEEP. Flow decelerates as alveolar and circuit pressures equilibrate. Vt is NOT set — it is a consequence of ΔP, compliance, and resistance
CycleTime (Ti)Ti is directly set and guaranteed. Breath ends when the timer expires, regardless of volume delivered. I:E ratio is under direct clinician control

Clinical pearls: Driving pressure (ΔP = Pplat − PEEP) is a key predictor of ARDS mortality — PC makes ΔP immediately visible as the set parameter. Critical caveat: if compliance improves (e.g., prone positioning), Vt can increase dramatically — monitor closely.

Assist-Control (A/C)
History: A/C emerged in the 1970s as an evolution of controlled mandatory ventilation (CMV). CMV delivered only time-triggered breaths — patients who tried to breathe spontaneously between machine breaths would "fight" the vent. A/C solved this: every patient effort that crosses the trigger threshold delivers a full assisted breath at the same set parameters as the mandatory breath.
A/C is a trigger strategy, not a standalone mode — it is applied to either VC or PC:
Full nameTriggerLimitCycle

Time-trigger fires if patient rate falls below the set backup rate — ensures minimum minute ventilation even in apnoea.

Unlike SIMV, every patient-triggered breath receives full ventilator support at the set parameters — not a spontaneous unsupported breath.

Clinical pearls: Tachypnoea in A/C delivers full Vt with every breath → risk of breath stacking, dynamic hyperinflation, and respiratory alkalosis. Address the root cause of tachypnoea (pain, anxiety, acidosis) — don't just increase sedation.

Advanced modes: BiPAP · APRV · AVAPS
Non-invasive and complex modes decoded through the trigger · limit · cycle framework
NIV BiPAP — what it actually is
BiPAP delivers two alternating levels of CPAP: IPAP (inspiratory) and EPAP (expiratory). The driving pressure is ΔP = IPAP − EPAP. This is mechanically identical to Pressure Support Ventilation delivered through a non-invasive interface — EPAP functions as PEEP, IPAP is the pressure limit during inspiration.
PhaseVariableDetail
TriggerPatient flow effortFlow trigger — patient effort drops circuit flow below baseline. Backup time-trigger fires if apnoea occurs.
LimitPressure (IPAP)Circuit pressure limited to IPAP. Flow decelerates as lung pressure rises — identical waveform to PSV.
CycleFlow % of peakBreath ends when inspiratory flow decays to ~25–30% of peak. Machine drops back to EPAP. Adjustable as expiratory trigger sensitivity (ETS).
Leak compensation
Unlike invasive vents, NIV machines actively compensate for mask leak. Critical for maintaining EPAP and detecting true patient effort through a leaking circuit.
Rise time
Controls how quickly pressure rises from EPAP to IPAP. Faster = more flow early = better for high-demand patients (COPD, APE). Slower = more comfortable for OSA.
Expiratory trigger sensitivity (ETS)
The flow-cycle threshold. In COPD, flow decays slowly — low ETS causes delayed cycling (patient exhales against IPAP). Increasing ETS corrects this.
When the backup rate fires — the critical scenario
When patient's spontaneous rate falls below the backup rate, the ventilator switches from flow-cycled assisted breaths to time-cycled mandatory breaths:
SituationTriggerLimitCycle
Patient rate > backup ratePatient flowIPAPFlow % — patient controls Ti
Patient rate < backup rateTimeIPAPTime — set Ti controls breath

Clinical implication: The backup Ti is not a throwaway setting. When backup fires, BiPAP becomes Pressure Control. Set the backup Ti as deliberately as you would set Ti in a PC mode — typically 0.8–1.2 s for adults. Ask yourself: "If this patient becomes apneic overnight, what breath will they receive?"

Conceptual anchor: BiPAP on a dedicated NIV device = PSV through a mask with leak compensation. The leak-handling algorithm is the key engineering difference. The breath physics are identical.

Common misconception: "BiPAP" is a Respironics trade name. On other devices the same physiology may be called NIV-PS, BiLevel, or DuoPAP. Always decode: what is IPAP, EPAP, and how is cycling determined?

APRV — Airway Pressure Release Ventilation
APRV applies a prolonged high pressure (P-high, held for T-high, typically 4–6 seconds) to recruit and maintain lung volume, then briefly releases to a low pressure (P-low, held for T-low, typically 0.4–0.8 seconds) for CO₂ elimination. The patient breathes spontaneously throughout — primarily during P-high. Oxygenation is maintained by sustained high mean airway pressure; ventilation occurs during the release.
P-high (T-high ~5 s) — spontaneous breathing during this phase
Release (T-low ~0.5 s)
PhaseVariableDetail
TriggerTime (primarily)Transition from P-low back to P-high is time-triggered (T-low timer). Spontaneous breaths on P-high are patient-triggered, flow-cycled (PSV superimposed on CPAP).
LimitPressure (P-high / P-low)Both phases are pressure-limited. During T-high, P-high is the ceiling. During release, P-low is the floor (often 0 cmH₂O, but short T-low prevents full derecruitment).
CycleTime (T-high / T-low)T-high ends the sustained inflation phase; T-low ends the release. Both are time-cycled. APRV is mechanically a variant of time-cycled Pressure Control with an inverted I:E ratio (~10:1 to 12:1).
T-low — the most critical and most mismanaged parameter
T-low too long
Full exhalation → alveolar collapse → derecruitment. Defeats the purpose of APRV.
T-low appropriately short (0.4–0.8 s)
Terminate before collapse — residual gas maintains alveolar recruitment. The lung never fully deflates. Target: end T-low when expiratory flow has decayed to 50–75% of peak expiratory flow.
T-low too short
Insufficient CO₂ clearance; progressive gas trapping and intrinsic PEEP.

Flow-based T-low titration: Observe peak expiratory flow at the start of T-low on the flow waveform. End T-low when flow has decayed to ~50–75% of that peak. This is a dynamic target — retitrate as lung mechanics change.

History & controversy
APRV was described by Stock and Downs in 1987 as a way to support spontaneous breathing at high mean airway pressures. Proponents argue it recruits lung while preserving diaphragm activity and reducing sedation requirements. Critics note the absence of large RCTs demonstrating mortality benefit over lung-protective VC, and that poorly set T-low can worsen lung injury. It remains widely used but debated.

Contraindications / caution: Obstructive lung disease (air trapping), elevated ICP (high mean Paw impairs cerebral venous drainage), bronchopleural fistula, and absent spontaneous respiratory drive (spontaneous breathing is essential to APRV physiology).

AVAPS — Average Volume-Assured Pressure Support
AVAPS (Philips Respironics) — and equivalents iVAPS (ResMed), AutoTrak — is a dual-control mode combining the patient comfort of pressure support with volume targeting. Each individual breath is pressure-limited and flow-cycled (identical to PSV/BiPAP), but the pressure support level automatically adjusts breath-to-breath to achieve a target average tidal volume.
Two levels of control operating simultaneously:

Within each individual breath
TriggerPatient flow effortFlow-triggered, exactly as in BiPAP/PSV. Backup time trigger for apnoea.
LimitPressure (current PS level)IPAP is the pressure ceiling for that breath. Flow decelerates to maintain it.

Auto-titrationTarget VtAlgorithm compares measured Vt to target. Vt too low → PS increases. Vt too high → PS decreases. Rate: slow (~1–2 cmH₂O per few breaths) to avoid instability.
Key parameters
Target Vt
The volume the algorithm aims to achieve — an average over multiple breaths, not a per-breath guarantee. Set at 6–8 mL/kg IBW.
IPAP min / IPAP max
The allowable pressure range for auto-titration. Algorithm searches within this window. IPAP max is a safety ceiling against barotrauma.
EPAP
Set manually — not auto-titrated in standard AVAPS. Provides PEEP effect and stents upper airway. Critical for OSA-overlap patients.
Backup rate + Ti — critical when apnoea occurs
When backup fires, AVAPS switches to time-cycled PC at current IPAP. The Vt auto-titration algorithm may misinterpret changes during backup epochs and make erratic pressure adjustments if backup Ti is poorly matched to passive lung mechanics.
Clinical niche & history
AVAPS was developed around 2006 for chronic NIV in patients with variable or progressive respiratory mechanics — obesity hypoventilation syndrome (OHS), neuromuscular disease (ALS, Duchenne), and hypercapnic COPD. Patients with progressive NMD have declining compliance and strength — AVAPS automatically compensates without manual retitration at every follow-up visit.
Strongest evidence
OHS — RCTs (Masa et al., 2020; Howard et al., 2017) show non-inferiority to fixed-pressure NIV with potential adherence advantages.
Less useful acutely
The slow breath-to-breath titration algorithm is designed for chronic home use. For acute respiratory failure, fixed BiPAP or ICU PSV is more appropriate.

Conceptual anchor: AVAPS is to PSV what a thermostat is to a heater. Each breath (heater cycle) still runs identically — pressure-limited, flow-cycled. A higher-level controller (thermostat/algorithm) adjusts the pressure setting across breaths based on the gap between measured and target Vt. This "outer loop" is the dual-control concept — identical in principle to invasive PRVC/AutoFlow/VC+.

Key conceptual relationships
NIV BiPAP = PSV through a mask
Mechanically identical trigger-limit-cycle structure. The distinguishing features are the interface (no ETT), intentional leak, and leak-compensation algorithms.
APRV = PC with extreme I:E inversion
If you set PC with Ti = 5 s, Te = 0.5 s, P-high = 25, P-low = 0 — you have APRV. The mode name emphasizes the physiological rationale rather than the mechanics.
AVAPS = PSV with an outer control loop
Each breath is PSV. The algorithm adds a second control layer — adjusting PS level across breaths to chase a Vt target. Analogous to PRVC/AutoFlow/VC+ in invasive ventilation.
Invasive PRVC / AutoFlow / VC+ = AVAPS equivalent
Same dual-control concept invasively. Key difference: PRVC guarantees Vt per breath; AVAPS targets an average Vt over multiple breaths.

Universal decoder: When you encounter any unfamiliar mode name — on any ventilator, invasive or non-invasive — ask: (1) What starts the breath? (2) What is held constant during inspiration? (3) What ends the breath? Those three answers tell you everything about the breath's mechanics, regardless of the proprietary name on the screen.
